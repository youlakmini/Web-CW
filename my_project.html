Sri Lanka's Mangrove Restoration Efforts Recognized as UN World Restoration Flagship

Learning from the devastating Tsunami 20 years ago, Sri Lanka is on track to bring back 10,000 hectares of mangroves by 2030. Mangrove forests play critical roles in Sri Lanka’s economy and climate resilience but are rapidly degrading due to climate change and pollution. A large-scale initiative to increase mangrove cover across Sri Lanka by more than 50 per cent was today named as one of seven UN World Restoration Flagships. The World Restoration Flagship awards are part of the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration – led by the UN Environment Programme (UNEP) and the Food and Agriculture Organization of the UN (FAO) – which aims to prevent, halt, and reverse the degradation of ecosystems on every continent and in every ocean. The awards track notable initiatives following global commitments to restore one billion hectares – an area larger than China

Importance of Mangrove Forests in Coastal Protection and Ecosystem Health

Mangrove forests thrive along the border between land and sea and are the first line of defence for coastlines, reducing erosion from storm surges, currents, waves, and tides. Their intricate root system makes them attractive to fish and other organisms seeking nurseries, food, and shelter. This need for the ecosystem’s mitigating effect was dramatically stressed during the 2004 tsunami, which killed more than 30,000 people in Sri Lanka. The award for the Sri Lanka Mangrove Regeneration initiative was announced by UNEP Advocate for Life Under Water and actor, Jason Momoa.

Adapting Strategies: Sri Lanka's Journey in Mangrove Restoration and Preservation

In the aftermath of the tsunami, Sri Lanka initially embarked on a large-scale mangrove planting effort, but faced significant challenges as only around three percent of the saplings survived. Learning from this, the country shifted its approach to focus on nurturing mangroves rather than solely planting them. By creating optimal conditions for natural regeneration, supported by scientific methods and community involvement, Sri Lanka has seen progress. Through partnerships with the Australian and UK governments, the initiative has restored 500 hectares of mangroves since its inception in 2015. Today, these resilient ecosystems serve as crucial buffers against climate change impacts like cyclones, floods, and coastal erosion, while also providing valuable resources such as medicinal plants, fish, and food security, especially for vulnerable communities. Sri Lanka recognizes the intrinsic value of mangroves, not only for its own well-being and prosperity but also as a contribution to global environmental priorities. Mr. B.K Prabath Chandrakeerthi, Secretary of the Ministry of Environment of Sri Lanka, emphasizes the importance of investing in restoration as a means to safeguard societal health, economic stability, and to honor the interconnectedness of all living beings with this vital ecosystem.

Sri Lanka's Comprehensive Approach to Mangrove Conservation and Restoration

In 2015, Sri Lanka became the first nation to legally protect all its mangrove forests and established the National Expert Committee on Mangrove Conservation and Sustainable Use, leading to various measures to safeguard and manage mangroves, including additional legal declarations. Building on this milestone, the Sri Lankan government adopted a National Policy on Conservation and Sustainable Utilization of Mangrove Ecosystems in 2020. To ensure the policy's effectiveness over time, the country developed the "National Strategic Action Plan for Conservation and Sustainable Utilization of Mangroves in Sri Lanka" in 2022, alongside National Guidelines. In 2022, Sri Lanka played a key role in sponsoring a UN resolution to designate March 1st as World Seagrass Day, emphasizing the interconnectedness between seagrasses and mangroves and their importance for ocean health. Recognized as a World Restoration Flagship, Sri Lanka's mangrove restoration endeavors are now eligible for technical and financial assistance from the UN, aiding the country's ambitious target of restoring 10,000 hectares of mangroves by 2030. This initiative aims to revive more than half of the nation's mangrove cover, benefiting numerous households and creating thousands of new employment opportunities.

Sri Lanka's Mangrove Restoration Efforts Earn Praise from UNEP Executive Director

Inger Andersen, Executive Director of UNEP, commended Sri Lanka's steadfast dedication to mangrove restoration, emphasizing the immense productivity of these ecosystems. She highlighted Sri Lanka's continuous efforts in refining mangrove planting techniques as a testament to the long-term commitment required for successful restoration initiatives. Andersen suggested that other nations could learn valuable lessons from Sri Lanka's experience. Sri Lanka anticipates achieving its restoration goals by 2030 with an additional investment of $3.5 million. This funding will support livelihood activities, maintenance of restoration sites, channel restoration, cleaning, boundary management, awareness campaigns, and the assessment of carbon sequestration value resulting from mangrove restoration.

Sri Lanka's Mangrove Restoration Named as a World Restoration Flagship

Sri Lanka's mangrove restoration, honored as a World Restoration Flagship, stands out as an exemplary model of extensive and enduring ecosystem revitalization on a global scale. It embodies the core principles of restoration, showcasing excellence in this field. The announcement of this recognition, along with six other new World Restoration Flagships, preceded the 6th UN Environment Assembly (UNEA-6), scheduled from February 26 to March 1, 2024. This assembly will bring together Environment Ministers worldwide in Nairobi, Kenya, to collectively address the interconnected challenges of climate change, biodiversity loss, and pollution.
